## Module Two: Manoeuvres

## Degree Of Difficulty Of Manoeuvres

The definitions in this module will facilitate a
clear decision, at the time of the ride, as to the
nature certain manoeuvres and their influence
on point allocation and scoring. For example,
a clear distinction is made between major
manoeuvres; re-entry’s, cutbacks, floaters,
carves, tube rides and aerials.
The transitional & minor manoeuvres – minor
examples of major manoeuvres, mid-face turns,
pump turns, speed turns and white water turns.
Furthermore, point allocation is affected by the
degree of difficulty that must be individually
categorised for each type of manoeuvre.
The degree of difficulty is defined in terms of
excellent, good, average, poor and bad.
Other aspects to consider when judging a surfer’s
ride across all surfing disciplines are:

## The Take-Off:

The take-off is the beginning of a ride when the
hands leave the rails and the surfer has made an
attempt to stand. The take-off is from the time of
hands leaving rail to the commencement of the first
manoeuvre to turn. Categorised by nature of the
wave and the point of entry in relation to each wave
and the degree of control.
1. Excellent (these occur rarely): Tube/cover-
up take-off with a smooth transition to first
manoeuvre or a “free fall”, which is a very steep
late take-off with a controlled transition into the
first manoeuvre can sometimes amount to an
excellent score.
2. Average (most falls into this category): Take-
off allows a smooth transition into the first
manoeuvre.
3. Poor: Off-balance or leads to disruption of
balance resulting in a delay of transition to first
manoeuvre or too far out onto the shoulder of
the wave.
The Bottom turn:
A bottom turn is a banking turn off the bottom
of the wave and is used to generate vertical
momentum and speed. It is a crucial aspect for
any manoeuvre that is performed off the top of the
wave. This is not scored as a Major manoeuvre, but
the bottom turn is essential to the surfer performing
manoeuvres with a high degree of difficulty. The
difficulty is determined by the angle of ascent and
the vertical momentum achieved.

The wave size:
The size of the wave can be a contentious area,
with some declaring the size of the wave is
comparable to the back of the wave. In this modern
era of our sport and with surfers continuing to
push the boundaries we are seeing extreme waves
being ridden that cannot be compared by the back
of the wave. Generally, the wave size is measured
relative to the person’s body height when the first
opportunity occurs to compare the standing height
of the person against the wave face – generally
the first bottom turn. Size can be classified as
overhead, double overhead, etc.

The critical sections:
In modern competitions, the judges should be more
focused on the critical sections and how heavy
these sections are. In varying wave conditions, the
heaviest sections may not necessarily be the first
sections of the wave.
Manoeuvres that are performed with relative
elements of the criteria in the heaviest sections
should always be rewarded by the judging panel.

## Major Manoeuvres

Major Manoeuvres are the most outstanding or
eye-catching manoeuvres that most influence the
judge’s score.

Tube or barrel ride:
Clean entry and exit, depth and length of time in
the tube will determine the degree of difficulty.
Manoeuvres in the tube that are used as a stalling
technique also have a bearing on the score.
Crumbling tubes can score less than clean dry
tubes.
Aerial:
Major re-entry manoeuvres that are projected past
the lip line with or without rotation in the air. Each
aerial varies in degrees of difficulty depending on
height, rotation, use of hands-on the board and
clean landings.
The classification of all aerials will determine the
degree of difficulty and the technical aspects of
the manoeuvre. These are generally categorised
dependent on their technicality.
• Technical: Average to good scoring range
• Very technical: High good to excellent scoring
range

Re-entry:
A top turn where the rider re-enters the wave face
after contacting the lip or breaking white water.
A re-entry is measured by steepness and size of
the angle of ascent/descent. The most difficult
re-entry is to go past vertical in ascent and/or to
cross over the path of the original vertical ascent
when descending; in other words, in a figure-eight
direction or path.

Cutback:
A horizontal direction change of at least 130°, in
which the board reverses its original path so that it
returns towards the breaking part of the wave; this
type of manoeuvre usually precedes the rebound
off the white-water. The most difficult cutback is the
roundhouse; this means that the surfer extends far
out into the shoulder of the wave and with no loss
of speed, return at 180° in a figure of eight paths
into the breaking curl with a high rebound off the
white water.

Tail slides:
A tail slide is a variation of re-entry or carve and
has a higher degree of difficulty. The surfer pushes
the surfboard releasing the fins with control; the
degree of difficulty is indicated by where the surfer
performs the slide and how close to the critical
section. Tail slides performed with speed can
extend from 90-degrees to the full 360-degrees
which are called Tail slide into a reverse.
Floater:
This manoeuvre requires the board to be projected
up onto the breaking white water or lip and to
traverse the section horizontally before descending
into the unbroken face. Some variations to a
standard floater are a tailslide floater or a ‘rock
n roll’ floater, where the board descends tail
first down the breaking wave whilst control is
maintained. A free fall from this position indicates a
high degree of difficulty.

Top Turn/carve:
A turn performed in the top one-third of the wave
but does not extend into the lip of the wave as in
re-entry and does not reverse horizontal direction
by more than 130°. A major scoring top-turn/
carve will show a lot of spray showcasing a very
important factor - POWER.

## Minor Manoeuvres

Minor manoeuvres are smaller safer versions of
major manoeuvres and also depend upon the
degree of difficulty and their functionality in the
context of the wave as a whole.

Foam turns/Floater:
Foam turns or foam climbs are generally only
a pleasing factor in poor surf conditions, as an
alternative to riding straight or groveling.
A foam floater covers distance and originates and
ends in the white-water. It enhances white-water
riding potential as an alternative to trimming or
hopping.

## Transitional Manoeuvres

Transitional manoeuvres are turns that link Major
manoeuvres together to form a visually appealing
aspect to the entire ride Transitional Manoeuvres
very rarely have a major reflection on the overall
score for the ride.

Mid-face Turns:
Minor direction changes, i.e. between 60° and
130° that have little influence on scoring potential.

Fade:
A motion after takeoff that directs the board
towards the curl before the bottom turn. The fade
can be used to accentuate a bigger top turn or
used as a stalling manoeuvre.

Pump Turns:
The un-weighting or hopping motion that generates
forward momentum in a slow section of the wave.
They lack the scoring potential of other turns
because they maintain horizontal direction and
don’t make a significant climbing or dropping
direction change; i.e. less than 60°.
Trimming:
Trimming is constant forward speed without
apparent direction change that carries no scoring
potential and may be detrimental to score if
construed as a missed opportunity. This can be on
the face or in the white water.
Efforts, such as hopping or wiggling, involved in
maintaining momentum in the white water, this
carries no scoring value. However, this can lead
to other sections of the wave where the surfer
may continue to ride the green face where scoring
potential can be added.

Stalling:
It is a deliberate weight-shift to the rear of the
board, to slow its momentum. Occasionally stalling
can be classified as a mistake.

Recovery:
Re-standing after losing footing is not a scoring
factor, as it indicates a mistake has been made.
This can neutralise the scoring potential of this
manoeuvre.

## Method Of Finish

1. Controlled:
- Kick Out:
• Deliberate exit over the back of the wave,
maintaining board control.
- Pull Out:
• Exit through the bottom of the wall of the
wave with board and body moving in the
same direction.
- Step Off:
• A stall or exit at the end of the wave or
when reaching the shoreline.
2. Uncontrolled:
- Any wipe out:
• Fall off or ungainly exit resulting in loss
of board control. Any manoeuvre where
the surfer falls before its completion is a
wipeout and does not enhance the wave
score.
- Prone Out:
• A deliberate return to the prone position as
an alternative to losing footing or control.
Proning out is generally detrimental to
scoring potential.
