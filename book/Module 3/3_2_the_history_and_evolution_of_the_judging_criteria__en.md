## The History & Evolution Of The Judging Criteria

“As the surf consists of a number of waves,
of which every third is remarked to be always
much larger than the others, and to flow
higher onto the shore, the rest breaking in the
intermediate space, their first object of the
natives is to place themselves on the summit
of the largest surge, by which they are driven
along with amazing rapidity toward the shore.
The boldness and address of which I saw
them perform these difficult and dangerous
manoeuvres, was altogether astonishing, and is
scarcely to be credited. ”
The above paragraph is an extract from the log of
Captain Cook’s ship in 1784: made by Lt King,
who was in command of the ship, after Cook was
killed by the natives in Hawaii.
This extract refers to the waves and then to
the skill of the local natives which were riding
the waves. Power (biggest wave), Speed,
Commitment, Degree of Difficulty all mentioned
in the ships log, which is a historical document

Is this the first judging criteria?
The surf judging criteria has for most of its
existence been very “subjective” with none of the
benefits of the “first past the post” sports to decide
a definitive winner. Since the inception of surfing
contests in the early 1960’s the need to evaluate
a surfers’ performance was required to crown a
winner.
Judging a surf contest is basically a matter of
opinion. From the beginning of our contest

activities in the early 60’s, a judging criteria has set
the standard for our contest surfers to meet.
At the ISA founding meeting in Peru Feb 23, 1965
the original and formal judging criteria was agreed
on by all present after much deliberation and
discussion.

“A surfer will be judged and given the maximum
points for sliding at the greatest speed,
the longest distance possible, in the most
dangerous part of the wave, with the greatest
functional manoeuvrability”.
It is interesting to note as it uses the critical
(dangerous) part of the wave, speed, manoeuvres
with highest degree of difficulty and longest ride
descriptions.
As our sport evolved the need to adjust the judging
criteria became apparent. At this time and up until
the early 1980’s all scores per ride were based on
the 20 point system below:
0 to 4 – Poor ride. 5 to 9 – Average ride.
10 to 14 – Good ride. 15 to 20 – Excellent ride.
Before the start of the World Surfing
Championships in 1970, the International Surfing
Association amended the International Judging
definitions and criteria at the insistence of the
Australian delegation. The judging criteria was
amended to read:
“Riders will be given maximum points for
performing in the most difficult part of the
wave, for the longest time, at the fastest speed
possible, displaying a wider variety of functional
manoeuvres involving the highest degree of
difficulty. A quality larger wave is better than a
smaller quality wave”
The onset of professional surfing started around
1973 with Rip Curl sponsoring the first Bells
Beach Easter Pro, and a few years later in 1976
the International Professional Surfers Association
(IPS) was formed. As performance levels and prize
money within the sport increased so to did the
importance of judging and the criteria.

Points per manoeuvre system:
The only decisive change from the “criteria
subjective” system was a very brief flirtation in the
mid 70’s with a “points per manoeuvre” system
which was designed to eliminate the subjectivity of
the sport which was seen as a problem in judging
at the time.

Below is a brief description of the points per
manoeuvre system.
The beach was divided into zones of takeoff points
often reflecting wave height. Each takeoff area
provided various degrees of difficulty. Manoeuvres
were allocated a point value reflecting in their
degree of difficulty. Each competitor had an
individual judge who nominated the moves and the
points for the wave was multiplied by the factor for
the takeoff zone, giving the final score.
Without computers the calculations were a
nightmare. The nomination of the surfers moves
and takeoff zone on the beach, were to some
extent also subjective and in the hands of individual
judges.
After this short unsuccessful trial over a couple of
events, it was still decided that averaging points
scores over a panel of expert judges was proven to
be the most reliable method of evaluating a surfer’s
performance.
As the sport continued through the early years
of professionalism the criteria was seen to be
expressive of the current state of the sport and the
performance levels. It is interesting to note that this

was when the biggest changes came to equipment
and surfboards. The single fin era gave way to
multi finned surfboards and light weight materials.
However the judging criteria remained stagnant for
10 years or so.
The biggest change to judging in this era was the
implementation of the 10 point scoring scale using
decimals in the mid 80’s.
However the use of.1,.4,.6 &.9’s were not
allowed to be used when scoring as it was thought
the judges could not differentiate the surfers rides
to this smallest degree.
It was also thought that it would be a positive rule
as this automatically opened each judges scoring
scale. The scale that was used is as below:
0.1 to 2.0 – Bad ride. 2.0 to 4.0 – Poor ride.
4.0 to 6.0 – Average ride. 6.0 to 8.0 – Good ride.
8.0 to 10.00 – Excellent ride.

“Riders will be given maximum points for
performing in the most difficult parts of the
wave, selected for quality and size, for the
longest time, at the fastest speed using
the widest range of functional manoeuvres
involving the highest degree of difficulty”
The decade to come after these drastic changes
to equipment were the 1990’s and was largely
seen as the modern short board tri-fin era. The
changes to the Judging criteria were continuous
and reflected the diverse changes of the sport and
the performance levels of the surfers.
“The surfer who executes the most radical
controlled manoeuvres in the most critical
section of the biggest and/or best waves with
the most speed and the highest degree of
difficulty for the longest functional distance
shall be deemed the winner”

“A surfer must execute the most radical
controlled manoeuvres in the critical section
of a wave with speed and power throughout.
The surfer who executes such manoeuvres on
the biggest and or best waves for the longest
functional distance shall be deemed the winner”

“A surfer must execute the most radical
controlled manoeuvres in the critical section
of a wave with speed and power throughout.
The surfer who executes such manoeuvres on
the biggest and or best waves for the longest
functional distance shall be given the higher
scores”
The 2000’s started with big changes to the
wording of the criteria. It was felt by the officials
and the top elite surfers that drastic changes had
to be made. The last years of the 90’s showed that
a new era of futuristic surfing was coming with
inclusion of aerials and futuristic manoeuvres that
were only being performed very rarely by a handful
of elite surfers of the sport.
The time was right to make these changes to the
criteria and was by far, the biggest step forward

with the judges being able to reward surfers that
were setting the future standards and delivering
exciting performances, in competitions.
Around this time there was the elimination of the
amount of scoring waves in a surfer’s final total.
Over the past decades the best four waves and
then the best three waves were counted. It was
decided in a very simplistic way that going to
the best two scoring waves would eliminate the
average performing surfer. It was decided that if a
surfer only needed to score two waves to count in
the end total, this would allow them to go for high-
risk manoeuvres more often in a heat.
In 1999 professional judges needed to use every
decimal point when trying to differentiate between
the surfers. The scoring scale was adjusted to
show clear separation between the descriptions
and scoring values. Heats and performance levels
were getting closer and the use of the full 99 point
scoring scale became apparent. The scale used is
still current today and is as below:
0.1 to 1.9 – Bad ride. 2.0 to 3.9 – Poor ride.
4.0 to 5.9 – Average ride. 6.0 to 7.9 – Good ride.
8.0 to 10.00 – Excellent ride.

“A surfer must perform committed radical
manoeuvres in the most critical sections of a
wave with style, power and speed to maximise
scoring potential. Innovative and progressive
surfing will be taken into account when
rewarding points for committed surfing.

The surfer who executes this criteria with the
highest degree of difficulty and control on the
better waves shall be rewarded with the higher
scores”
“A surfer must perform committed radical
manoeuvres in the most critical sections of a
wave with speed, power and flow to maximise
scoring potential. Innovative and progressive
surfing as well as a variety of repertoire
(manoeuvres) will be taken into account when
rewarding points for waves ridden.

The surfer who executes this criteria with the
highest degree of difficulty and commitment
on the waves shall be rewarded with the higher
scores”
The latest changes to the surf judging criteria show
more emphasis on the performance of the surfer on
the waves and not the quality of the waves.

“A surfer must perform radical controlled
manoeuvres in the critical sections of a wave
with speed, power and flow. Innovative/
progressive surfing as well as a combination
and variety of repertoire (of major manoeuvres)
will be taken into account when rewarding
points for a surfers’ performance.

The surfer who executes the criteria above
exhibiting the maximum degree of difficulty and
commitment on the waves shall maximise his/
her scoring potential”

The current Shortboard criteria is as follows:
Commitment and Degree of Difficulty
Innovative and Progressive manoeuvres
Combination of Major manoeuvres
Variety of manoeuvres
Speed, Power and Flow
