## Module One: Snapshot Of A Good Judge

Being a surf judge is both a fulfilling and
challenging experience. If you have a passion
for both surfing and personal development,
then judging is a great pathway for you to
accomplish your goals.
As a judge, you have an opportunity to positively
impact individuals in surfing – both competitors
and participants associated with the sport. You
could be a volunteer at your local Boardriders club
or working in a professional capacity at a surfing
competition.
With this outcome front of mind, in a nutshell, what
does a judge do? A judge is the key person who
officiates at competitions and ensures sound and
ethical scoring practices are applied and adapted
depending on the age and ability of participants.
As a judge, the following characteristics are key to
your role:
Ability to focus.
To remain impartial and keep consistency in your
scoring decisions.
Confidence in the training you have received
and knowledge you have acquired in your
professional journey. Diplomacy, tact and
common sense are crucial.
Recognising and acknowledging the lessons
learnt to help professional growth.
This role also offers many personal development
opportunities. Working with the head judge and
other colleagues to improve your skills can promote
consistency in judging and increase participant
satisfaction.
Furthering your education to become a head judge,
we encourage development by completing ‘Elite
Judging’ course modules and to continue your

professional development. Advancing your studies
may also open up opportunities to judge nationally
and internationally.
When judging, you will be required to concentrate
and often have to make consistent decisions under
pressure. Judges are required to make a series
of subjective decisions based on performance, or
the technical aspects of surfing, as part of their
job. This is often in a short space of time. Outside
noise from spectators, coaches or teams can add
pressure and lead to further confusion around
the final decision. To combat this, you need to
remain calm and confident in your decision-making.
Confidence will be generated by improving your
knowledge around the role and criteria.

## What Does A Judge Need To Know?

Knowledge of Surfing’s Rules and Criteria
Some people would say it is obvious, but a good
understanding of surfing’s rules and criteria is
critical to your role. How often do you check
surfing’s rules and criteria? Regularly referring
to your rule book to keep the rules fresh in your
mind and bringing it to competitions in case you
ever need to refer to the criteria ensure your
professional capabilities.

Understanding Legal Responsibilities
As a judge, make sure you have thoroughly
checked the legal responsibilities outlined in the
judging manual. Understanding what is expected
of you by your National Sporting Organisation and
what legal responsibilities you have while judging
and officiating are vital to your success. Always
refer to the Officials Code of Behaviour in the
current rule book.
Professional Attitude
As a representative of surfing, it is important
to show a professional attitude when judging.
This includes being punctual, prepared, wearing
appropriate attire, being fair and respectful to
participants and spectators.

Surfing Ambassador
As a judge, you will often be a professional public
face of surfing. It is important to remember that
you may be the person who competitors will come
into contact with for the first time. Be mindful of
the way that you represent yourself and surfing
and remember that your actions can influence a
participant’s future involvement in surfing.

Understanding Safety and Risk
Judges have a duty to take reasonable care to
avoid injury to participants, and this includes
keeping up to date with the rules of surfing,
particularly rules relating to safety aspects. When
judging, it is important to always adapt and mitigate
risks against the changing conditions. Always refer
to the Risk management policies in the current rule
book.

Preparation is Key
Always make sure you are prepared for your role,
and that you keep a consistent judging scale in a
confident and timely manner. Arrive early, be sure
you are dressed appropriately and make sure you
are protected from the sun and other weather.

Fairness
There will be times when you’ll be put under
pressure from participants, coaches and
spectators when making decisions. This pressure
can be tough, but it is important that you maintain
your integrity and confidence, and make fair and
impartial decisions. Remaining open minded to the
head judge is also key.

Self-Improvement
Other ways you can improve as an official include:
Participating in discussions with your head judge,
pursuing experience and commitment, regular self-
reflection and completion of an elite-level coaching
course.
