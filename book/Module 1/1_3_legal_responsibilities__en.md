## Legal Responsibilities

As a judge, you have a responsibility to enforce
the rules or laws of your sport. This helps
protect participants, ensure that the sport can
be conducted in a safe manner, controls and
supervises the competition and warns participants
whose actions may be putting others in danger.
This is a judge’s ‘duty of care’ – a critical element
to your role.
It is a good idea to contact your sporting
organisation or member organisation, and check
what your sport expects of you with regards to your
duty of care and how this may translate into any
legal responsibilities. You should also explore what
insurance is available to you.

Duty of Care
Everyone has a legal duty to take reasonable care
to make sure that nothing they do causes injury to
another person, or causes an injury by failing to
do something. If an injury arises from normal and
reasonable practice that is ‘part of the sport’, there
is no breach of duty. However, if an injury arises
because someone neglected to meet the standard
of care required of their role, it may be determined
that they have breached their duty.

Standard of Care
The standard of care that a judge must show is
the standard of a disciplined official with a suitable
level of training. In other words, you must do
what is reasonable to make sure that participants
compete safely, and within the rules.

Negligence
Negligence is a legal term that applies if somebody
breaches a standard of care. This is something
that will not impact you in your role if you take
reasonable care and carry out your judging
duties in a professional manner. However, it is an
important term to understand. Negligence arises
out of a breach of the standard of care. There must
be a link established between the actions of a
person, and the injury of another, for ‘causation’ to
be established.

Discrimination and Harassment
Discrimination is treating a person less favourably
than someone else on the basis of an attribute
or personal characteristic. Harassment is
where someone uses pressure, intimidation,
aggressive behaviour, teasing, jokes, exclusion
or unreasonable demands to force someone into
behaving or acting against their wishes.
There are a number of statutes in your country
that make discrimination, harassment and sexual
harassment unlawful. Your role as an official is
to comply with these laws, and to report any
behaviour that you believe falls under these
categories. Severe consequences will result for
people who conduct themselves in such a manner.

Sexual Harassment
Sexual harassment includes unwanted sexual
advances, unwelcome requests for sexual favours,
or unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature. Any
sexual advances by an adult towards a child (legally
under 18 years old), constitutes child sexual abuse.
Any cases should be immediately reported to the
appropriate authorities.
