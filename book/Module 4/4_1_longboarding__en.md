## Longboarding

The popularity of longboarding has created a
need to develop a judging philosophy and a
specialised criteria.
The principle is to recreate a typical riding method
on relatively standardised equipment which has the
dimensions of 9 feet long with a minimum of 47
inches in aggregate width.
In competition judges must use clear definitions
which facilitate a decision at the time of the ride
and to the nature of certain manoeuvres and their
influence on point allocation and scoring.
Furthermore, scoring and point allocation is
affected by the degree of difficulty that must
be individually categorised for each type of
manoeuvre.
The degree of difficulty is defined in terms of
excellent, good, average, poor and bad.

For example, a clear distinction must be made
between major manoeuvres such as nose rides,
re-entry’s, carves, cutbacks, floaters, tube rides as
well as any traditional variations that may showcase
a higher degree of difficulty such as, drop-knee
cutbacks and carves, fade take-offs and bottom
turns prior to major manoeuvres and clean cross
stepping through transitions and manoeuvres.
Having a lower degree of difficulty and a lesser
scoring value is the minor and transitional
manoeuvres. These are minor examples of major
manoeuvres, stretch nose rides, mid-face turns,
pump turns, speed turns and white water turns.
The below criteria are the key words of the
Longboard criteria that need to be considered by
the judges when scoring a surfer’s ride.
Using the key words of the judging criteria and the
when deliberating on a score will showcase exactly
what constitutes good surfing and that the surfers
and spectators know the points on which the
competitors are going to be judged.

## Longboard Judging Criteria

The Longboard surfer must perform controlled
manoeuvres in the critical section of the
wave utilising the entire board and wave using
traditional longboard surfing.
The surfer who performs this to the highest degree
of difficulty with the most style, flow and grace
will receive the highest score for a ride.
Further to that above, the following are key
elements for the judges to consider:
Nose riding and rail surfing
Critical section of the wave
Variety
Speed and Power
Commitment
Control
Footwork
Controlled traditional manoeuvres performed in
the critical sections have a direct bearing to quality
longboard surfing.
The degree of difficulty, commitment and risk
involved in performing close to the critical section.
determines a higher score. Recognising the
different types of manoeuvres and the degree of
difficulty is of the highest importance.
Variety of manoeuvres with speed and power and
the use of the entire length of the longboard are
required for good to excellent scoring rides. Variety
of manoeuvres highlights the need to present

different types of manoeuvres on the one wave,
the choice of manoeuvres with respect to critical
section indicate the rider’s skill.
How the surfer reads the wave and performing
manoeuvres that fit to the wave. Stylish, flowing
and graceful traditional elements with visual appeal
will become obvious if the surfer is committing to
the entire criteria.
The criteria are quite appropriate, provided
attention is made to the following:
Judges should consider the difference of
nose rides for example stretch or cheater fives
compared to real hang fives and tens.
Walking cleanly and precisely foot over foot to
the nose is superior to shuffling forward and
back along the board.
Style and flow with visual appeal are highlighted
by the way the surfer is able to link manoeuvres
together with no loss of control or speed.
Cross stepping in and out of turns and nose
rides is difficult and adds a definite visual appeal
to a surfers’ ride.
Judges should appreciate carving drop knee
turns and extended nose rides with the weight
directly over the tip of the nose.

## Judging Tip

Judges should always score the manoeuvres
not the quality of the wave or length of ride.
To perform manoeuvres that will be rewarded
highly by the judges, the surfer must commit to
performing manoeuvres which have a high degree
of difficulty and risk in the critical sections with
control.
The judges must always consider the degree of
difficulty shown by a surfer when fully completing a
manoeuvre.
A major movement in the Long board community
and with modern competition surfing are the
traditional elements of the criteria. Surfers will
score highly when complying to this area of the
criteria especially if executed with style and grace
with clean footwork in transitions between major
scoring manoeuvres.

## Nose Rides

Judges should consider the difference in
accomplishment in nose rides, e.g. stretch or
cheater fives as compared to real hang fives and
hang tens.

The surfer’s weight should be positioned over the
nose which has a higher degree of difficulty. The
best nose rides are always performed in the critical
section and are ‘held’ for a reasonable amount of
time. Maintaining speed and control in the critical
section for longer periods of time is the reason that
nose riding can score highly.
Classic nose rides are best when the walk to the
nose is set up by a tail stall or directly in from the
arc of a turn.
An excellent nose ride is when the surfer can
transition directly to the nose by cross-stepping
and then holding a clean five to a hang ten with
their body weight distributed over the nose for
longer periods of time.

## Tube Or Barrel Ride

A clean entry and exit, depth and length of time
in the tube will determine the degree of difficulty.
Clean tubes rides for any real length of time
are quite rare in Longboarding and is usually
determined by a particular type of wave and
conditions.
Crumbling tubes will generally score less than
clean dry tubes, and judges should not over score
head dips or cover-ups

## Re-Entry

A re-entry is a top turn where the rider re-enters
the wave face after contacting the lip or breaking
white water. Difficulty is determined by the angle of
ascent and the vertical momentum achieved.
Most re-entries performed in competition are
usually more lateral in ascent and descent as the
length of the long board usually does not allow for
extreme vertical re-entry’s which are positioned
close to the critical sections.

## Carve

A turn performed in the top one-third of the wave
but does not extend into the lip of the wave as in a
re-entry and does not reverse horizontal direction
by more than 130°. Carves are usually performed
to slow the surfer down with some scoring value.
As opposed to standing on the tail to stall the
longboard. Look for traditional variations such as a
drop-knee carve or carve to switch stance.
The bottom turn is a crucial aspect for any
manoeuvre that is performed off the top of the
wave.

Judges should be mindful of fade take-offs and
bottom turns. The fade can be used to accentuate
a bigger top turn or used as a stalling manoeuvre.
Longboarders use the fade technique to allows
them time to set up a major scoring re-entry, top
turn or correct positioning for an extended nose
ride.

## Cutback

Is a horizontal direction change of at least 130°, in
which the board reverses its original path so that it
returns towards the breaking part of the wave; this

type of manoeuvre usually precedes the rebound
off the white-water. The position of the board on
the rebound is important to overall score, a full
figure 8 path is called a Roundhouse cutback and
has the highest degree of difficulty of all cutbacks.

## Drop-Knee Cutback

A traditional variation of the cutback is the drop-
knee cutback in which the surfer will bend his back
leg in an effort to manoeuvre the longboard in a
tighter arc closer to the critical section.

## Layback Cutback

Another traditional variation of the cutback is the
lay-back cutback which is best performed close the
breaking part of the wave.
Judges need to be aware that a layback can be
a technique performed by a surfer to cover up a
mistake such as loss of control or speed.

## Floater

This manoeuvre requires the board to be
projected up onto the breaking white water or
lip and to traverse the section horizontally before
descending into the unbroken face or whitewater.
This is a relatively new manoeuvre in traditional
longboarding and is usually seen by the judges as
a functional and scoring way of using the breaking
section to gain access to the green face of the
wave again.

## Minor Manoeuvres

Minor manoeuvres are smaller safer versions of
major manoeuvres and also depend upon the
degree of difficulty and their functionality in the
context of the wave as a whole.
Face turns are usually flatter minor turns that
can generate speed and lead to bigger major
manoeuvres.

## Transitional Manoeuvres

Transitional turns link major manoeuvres together
to form a visually appealing aspect to the entire
ride and they rarely have a major reflection on the
overall score for the ride.

Key Elements for a judge to consider:
How well were the manoeuvres connected
together?
Did the surfer walk foot over foot along the
board or did they shuffle?
Are the surfer’s toes really hanging over or are
they back from the nose?
Has the surfer used the whole length of the
surfboard?
Consider how the surfer utilised and flowed with
the wave.
Did the surfer actually complete the manoeuvre
and with control?t
